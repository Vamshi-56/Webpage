What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cybercrime where targets are contacted by email, telephone, or text message by someone posing as a legitimate institution to lure individuals into providing sensitive data such as personally identifiable information, banking and credit card details, and passwords.

The information is then used to access important accounts and can result in identity theft and financial loss. The first phishing lawsuit was filed in 2004 against a Californian teenager who created the imitation of the website "America Online".

Types of Phishing Attacks

Cybercriminals use various phishing techniques to steal your information. Here are the most common types:

Email Phishing

Mass emails pretending to be from reputable companies (banks, tech firms) with urgent requests to verify accounts or update information.

Example: "Your account will be suspended unless you click here to verify your details."

Spear Phishing

Personalized attacks targeting specific individuals or organizations using gathered personal information to appear legitimate.

Example: An email to an HR manager appearing to come from the CEO requesting employee tax information.

Smishing & Vishing

Phishing via SMS (smishing) or voice calls (vishing) pretending to be from banks or government agencies.

Example: "Your bank card has been locked. Call this number immediately to unlock it."

Whaling

Targeted at high-profile individuals like CEOs (the "big fish") to access sensitive corporate data or authorize fraudulent transactions.

Example: An email to a CFO appearing to come from the CEO requesting an urgent wire transfer.

Clone Phishing

Legitimate emails that have been "cloned" with attachments or links replaced with malicious versions.

Example: A resent email you previously received, but with a "updated" malicious attachment.

Search Engine Phishing

Creating fake websites that appear in search results for popular products or services to steal payment info.

Example: A fake online store offering popular items at huge discounts to harvest credit card details.

Phishing Prevention Tips

Protect yourself with these essential security practices:

Hover over any link before clicking to see the actual URL. Look for:

  • Misspellings in the domain name (e.g., "paypai.com" instead of "paypal.com")
  • Strange subdomains (e.g., "paypal.security-update.com")
  • HTTP instead of HTTPS (though HTTPS alone doesn't guarantee safety)
  • Unusual characters or numbers in the URL

When in doubt, navigate directly to the website by typing the known URL in your browser.

MFA adds an extra layer of security beyond just passwords:

  • Enable MFA on all accounts that offer it (email, banking, social media)
  • Use authenticator apps (Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator) instead of SMS when possible
  • Consider hardware security keys for high-value accounts
  • Set up backup methods in case you lose access to your primary device

Even if your password is compromised, MFA can prevent unauthorized access.

The display name in emails can be faked. Always check the actual email address:

  • Look for slight variations from legitimate addresses (e.g., "support@amaz0n.com")
  • Be wary of free email domains (Gmail, Yahoo) used for official communications
  • Check for misspellings or added/omitted characters
  • Remember that legitimate companies rarely change their domain names

When in doubt, contact the company through their official website or customer service number.

Phishing often creates false urgency to bypass your critical thinking:

  • Be suspicious of emails threatening account closure or legal action
  • Legitimate companies won't demand immediate action via email
  • Watch for language designed to panic you ("Your account has been compromised!")
  • Verify any "urgent" requests through official channels

Take a moment to assess before reacting to alarming messages.

Regular updates protect against known vulnerabilities:

  • Enable automatic updates for your operating system
  • Keep browsers and plugins (Flash, Java) up to date
  • Update mobile apps regularly, especially security apps
  • Use reputable antivirus software with real-time protection

Many phishing attacks exploit known vulnerabilities that patches would fix.

Real Phishing Examples

Examine these real-world phishing attempts to recognize common patterns:

Email Phishing Financial

Fake PayPal Security Alert

Red Flags: Urgent language, generic greeting ("Dear User"), suspicious link (hover shows "paypal-security.net"), requests sensitive information.

How to Verify: Log in directly to PayPal (not via email link) to check for actual alerts. PayPal will address you by name in legitimate communications.

Tech Support Scam Vishing

Fake Microsoft Support Call

Red Flags: Unsolicited call claiming your computer has viruses, pressure to install remote access software, request for payment to "fix" non-existent problems.

How to Verify: Microsoft never makes unsolicited support calls. Hang up immediately. If concerned about your computer, contact Microsoft through official channels.

Document Phishing Account Takeover

Fake Google Docs Collaboration

Red Flags: Email appears to come from a known contact but with a fake Google Docs link, requests permission to access your Google account.

How to Verify: Check the sender's actual email address. Google Docs shares appear in your Drive, not as email attachments. Revoke suspicious app permissions at myaccount.google.com.
